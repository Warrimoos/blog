Some features get jokes written about them. This one doesn't.

Watching a pet have a seizure is frightening in a way that's hard to describe to people who haven't. Time goes strange. Thirty seconds feels like five minutes. Afterwards, when your vet asks perfectly reasonable questions, how long did it last, what did it look like, when was the last one, the honest answer is often "I have no idea, I was watching my dog".

That gap between what owners live through and what they can report is a real clinical problem, because epilepsy management runs on patterns. Whether seizures are getting more frequent. Whether they cluster. Whether the episode three weeks after a dose change means the new dose isn't holding. One seizure is a data point; the shape of six months of them is what medication decisions are actually made from.

So TreatMyPet2 has a seizure diary, and its design goal is different from every other screen in the app: be usable by someone whose hands are unsteady.

Logging in the moment takes two taps, which mostly just records the time, because the time is the thing memory distorts first. Everything else can wait. Later, when things are calm, you can go back and add how long it lasted, what you noticed, and anything odd about the hours before. The entry form deliberately asks its questions in plain words rather than clinical vocabulary, because the diary is for owners, and translating to vet language is my profession's job, not theirs.

Then the diary does its quiet work: it holds the timeline. At the next appointment, instead of reconstructing months from fragments, you hand over the actual record. Dates, times, durations, notes, alongside the medication history the app was already keeping. I have sat on the receiving end of both versions of that conversation, and the difference in what a vet can do with them is not small.

Two honest boundaries, stated plainly. A diary is not monitoring; the app cannot detect a seizure, and nothing about it replaces the phone call to your vet, especially the emergency one when an episode won't stop. And if your pet has never had a seizure, may you never open this screen. It's there for the households that need it, doing the same job a worn exercise book on the fridge used to do, just a little more legibly, and always in your pocket.

That's the whole feature. No badges, no streaks, no colour. Some parts of an app should simply be a good filing cabinet, and know it.
